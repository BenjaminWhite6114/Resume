Philosophy

Background

While I beleive I always had a philisophical impulse, I wasn't formally introduced to the subject until college. I owe my decision to switch to philosophy to who I would consider to be my mentor, and the intro level religious studies class I took with him. Though I only took the one class with him, we founded the George Mason Socratic Society together. His genuine love of deep discussion and his dedication to his students inspired me then, and continues to inspire me to this day.

Socratic Society

Socratic Society was a club dedicated to socratic discussion of topics ranging from semantics, to metaphysics, to theology. I was a founding member, and quickly took on the responsibilty of organizing the meetings and leading the discussions. During quarintine I moved the club online to a dedicated discord server, and we continued to do meetings in a hybrid style after quarintine ended.

Trasferable skills

Many of my most valuable skills come from studying and discussing philosophy. In socratic society, I learned a lot not just about leading a group, but also about communicating about topics which by there very nature are difficult to talk about. To have a productive conversation about such complex and intangible things requires one to be able to quickly understand wolrdviews vastly different from your own and to figure out how to bridge gaps in understanding on such basic levels as what it even means for something to be real. Studying philosophy in general require similar mental flexibility and swiftness in learning.

Reading

I don't want to make this section too long, so I'll leave off with my personal studies. A while back I decided I wanted to not just rely on schools and programs, but to continue my studies on my own so that even after graduation I'd continue to learn and improve. To show that, I've included a list of some of my recent reads (you can click on them for a description and my thoughts on each).

  • Camus is one of the most famous existentialists, and this is one of his most famous works. I think it's worth noting that Camus explicitely states at the beginning that he isn't trying to convince you of his base assumptions, so while his basic premise is the easiest point of attack in the work, Camus essentially says he isn't interested in convincing you of those anyway. Even with that weak foundation, he makes many interesting points.

  • This is arguably Camus's most famous work, and may even be one you had to read in school. I read it immediately after The Myth of Sisyphus, which is how I reccomend anyone else interested in the book approach it, as much of its symbolism is only clear if you're familiar with Camus's larger philosophy. I really loved this book. The amoral ambivelent tone of the narration surprisingly doesn't stop there from being intensly emotional moments, and seeing the narrator's total lack of reaction to them truly does make him seem a stranger to humanity. His otherness is palpable, leading to strange feelings about the arguable injustice he goes through later in the book.

  • This book is talks about both the history and mechanics of many of humanities most important inventions through the fun and whimsical framework of a stranded time traveler's guide to reinventing civilization. I feel like it actually helped fill in a lot of what felt like obvious gaps in my knowledge of both history and technology like what a fly-wheel is or how crop rotation actually works.

  • A bit of an outdated book, this one focuses on dispelling many economic myths of the 1990's by explaining some basic principles of economics as they're relevant to the United States, as well as using that information to provide a possible explaination of a trend in Us economics that does seem to still continue to the modern day. It both helped fill in gaps in my basic knowledge of economics, as well as give explainations of events I was only really vaugley aware of before.

  • Jean-Jacques Rousseau was one of the lead thinkers of the enlightenment movement that inspired the founding fathers. Political Philosophy has always been a weak point of mine, which is why I wanted to read this book. One interesting revelation is that, though presented as a justification of democracy when taught to me in highschool, The Social Contract does not endorse any form of government, instead explaining how different conditions favor different forms of government.

  • Hawking's most famous work explaining key features of both quantum and astrophysics, it certaintly does contain a lot of really interesting information, but I did have my frustrations with the book. I think they're best by the example of when he's explaining what particle spin is.The spin of a particle isn't its rotation, but how far it must be rotated to by symetrical. An arrow is symetrical only after being rotated 360 degrees, while a playing card may be symetrical after being rotated only 180 degrees. He then explains that strangely enough, some particals must be rotate MORE than 360 degrees in order to be symetrical! Would you be interested to know how that works? So would I, he just says that there is math proving it, not what that math is...
